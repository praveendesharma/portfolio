DSC 106 - Project 2 Persuasive or Deceptive Visualization?

Praveen Sharma — p4sharma@ucsd.edu

Proposition: Younger NYPD officers are more likely to be exonerated.

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Y-axis Truncation (0.04 to 0.058)
    • Rating: -2 (Highly Deceptive)
    • Rationale: Drastically exaggerates what is only a ~1% variation, making it appear more dramatic.
  • Color Choice (forestgreen)
    • Rating: -1 (Mildly Deceptive)
    • Rationale: Green implies approval/success, subtly associating youth with positivity.
  • Regression Smoothing (Lowess)
    • Rating: +1.5 (Mostly Earnest)
    • Rationale: Emphasizes general trend cleanly. No outlier distortion.
  • Highlighting Peak Zone with Shaded Region
    • Rating: -0.5 (Mildly Deceptive)
    • Rationale: Draws attention selectively to the most favorable range.
  • Title Language (“More Likely”)
    • Rating: -1 (Moderately Deceptive)
    • Rationale: “More likely” sounds substantial, though the magnitude of change is small.

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Use of Full Y-axis (0 to 0.6)
    • Rating: +2 (Earnest)
    • Rationale: Avoids exaggerating small fluctuations. Creates a stable, flat visual narrative in support of the counter-argument.
  • Neutral Color Choice (darkorange)
    • Rating: +1.5 (Mostly Earnest)
    • Rationale: Orange is attention-grabbing but not emotionally loaded like red or green. Keeps tone moderate and less persuasive.
  • Mean Line with Annotation
    • Rating: +1.5 (Mostly Earnest)
    • Rationale: Reinforces the central message of stability. Helps viewers orient their interpretation around a consistent baseline.
  • Framing of Age 41 Dip as Minor
    • Rating: -0.5 (Mildly Deceptive)
    • Rationale: Reinforces the central message of stability. Helps viewers orient their interpretation around a consistent baseline.
  • X-axis Range Restriction (22-45)
    • Rating: -1 (Moderately Deceptive)
    • Rationale: Focuses on a subset of ages where the data looks flatter, avoiding visible patterns at higher ages.

Final Reflection

Designing both plots required balancing clarity with persuasion. For the "neutral" plot, I intentionally used full y-axis scaling, a soft palette, and mean-line anchoring to convey stability and lack of trend. However, I still excluded older officers to avoid emerging trends — subtly deceptive but defensible in context. The persuasive plot, by contrast, uses several deceptive strategies: truncated y-axis, emotionally positive colors, and a shaded peak range to amplify a minor difference. Despite this, none of the visual choices are dishonest — they all exist on the spectrum of rhetorical framing. This project helped me realize that ethical visualization is not a binary. It's a craft of balance. Acceptable persuasive design is about subtle emphasis, not distortion. Misleading design, in contrast, breaks audience trust by obscuring structure or fabricating significance. The goal should be to guide interpretation, not to rig it.
